## 1. Purpose

Markets are competitive ecosystems.

If your strategy does not account for:
- Power asymmetry
- Competitor dominance
- Market narrative control
- Perception monopolies

Then it is not strategy.

It is decoration.

This engine forces the Agent to design positioning as controlled competitive pressure.

---

## 2. Core Principle

Positioning is not:
"What we say about ourselves."

Positioning is:
"What competitors are forced to say in response to us."

If your strategy does not alter competitor behavior,
it has no structural impact.

---

## 3. Competitive Landscape Mapping

The Agent must map competitors across 4 dimensions:

1. Authority Power
2. Distribution Power
3. Economic Power
4. Narrative Control

Output Required:

| Competitor | Authority | Distribution | Pricing Strength | Market Narrative | Weak Flank |

Weak Flank = Where they cannot defend.

## 4. Warfare Modes

The Agent must determine which mode applies:

Mode A – Flank Attack
Used when competitors are dominant.

Approach:
- Avoid direct competition.
- Enter through under-served segment.
- Create new narrative category.

Example:
Instead of "Best Insurance Provider"
→ Position as "Risk Intelligence Partner for Regulated Enterprises"

---

Mode B – Authority Hijack
Used when competitor dominates via brand name.

Approach:
- Amplify certifications.
- Publish executive-level thought leadership.
- Occupy conferences aggressively.
- Activate PR & analyst mentions.

Goal:
Shift perceived authority.

---

Mode C – Economic Undermining
Used when competitor competes on price.

Approach:
- Reframe cost to risk.
- Emphasize long-term exposure.
- Introduce ROI simulation tools.
- Highlight hidden cost of cheaper alternatives.

Never enter price war in high-ticket markets.

---

Mode D – Narrative Inversion
Used when market believes competitor is “default”.

Approach:
- Reframe buying logic.
- Highlight systemic flaws in default choice.
- Introduce doubt subtly.
- Offer safer alternative narrative.

---

Mode E – Category Redefinition
Used when competition is crowded.

Approach:
- Create new strategic frame.
- Rename the problem.
- Reclassify decision criteria.
- Force competitors into reactive position.

---

## 5. Competitive Pressure Points

The Agent must identify:

- Where competitor messaging is repetitive.
- Where they rely on outdated narrative.
- Where they lack local proof.
- Where they are slow in digital authority.
- Where they over-index on features.

These are structural cracks.

Strategy must enter through cracks, not front doors.

## 7. Defensive Layer Design

Every offensive positioning must include defense.

Agent must evaluate:

If competitor copies messaging:
→ What remains unique?

If competitor undercuts pricing:
→ What shifts our value perception?

If competitor increases ad spend:
→ Does authority layer hold?

If positioning collapses under replication,
it is weak.

## 9. Buying Committee Warfare Layer

Competitive positioning must influence:

Economic Buyer:
- Emphasize risk asymmetry.

Technical Buyer:
- Highlight architectural superiority.

Executive:
- Elevate strategic narrative.

Influencer:
- Create social proof pressure.

Goal:
Competitor becomes harder to justify internally.

---

## 10. Power Reallocation Strategy

Strategy must aim to:

- Increase share of voice.
- Increase authority visibility.
- Increase perceived stability.
- Increase trust density.

Without reducing competitor influence,
growth plateaus.

---

## 11. Strategic Red Flags

Positioning is weak if:

- It relies on generic claims.
- It avoids confrontation.
- It does not shift decision criteria.
- It competes on price.
- It cannot survive imitation.

---

## 12. Completion Condition

Competitive warfare strategy is complete when:

- Competitors are forced to react.
- Market narrative shifts slightly.
- Buying committee references your language.
- Your brand becomes part of evaluation checklist.
- Competitor is no longer default safe option.

If competitors remain unaffected,
strategy has no competitive impact.

End of Engine.
