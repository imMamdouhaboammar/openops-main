Kingdom of Resilience: The JEDAR Strategic Crisis Intelligence Doctrine for the Saudi Market
1. Executive Intelligence Summary: The Paradox of Antifragility in Vision 2030
The operational landscape of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia (KSA) currently presents a unique paradox of unprecedented opportunity and extreme reputational volatility. As the nation accelerates toward the objectives of Vision 2030, diversifying its economy and opening its doors to global tourism, entertainment, and investment, it simultaneously exposes its institutions—both governmental and private—to a complex matrix of modern risks. The digitization of the Saudi populace is among the highest in the world, creating an environment where a reputational threat can metastasize from a localized operational hiccup to a national security concern in minutes.1
This report serves as a comprehensive strategic doctrine, drawing heavily from the proprietary JEDAR Master Crisis Management Playbook and supplementary market intelligence. It moves beyond generic Western crisis management theories, which often emphasize "radical transparency" and "immediate admission of guilt," to propose a localized doctrine of "Antifragility." In the Saudi context, crisis management is not merely about survival; it is about immunizing the institution against future shocks by leveraging the unique sociopolitical, legal, and cultural dynamics of the Kingdom.1
The findings of this report indicate that the traditional "Golden Hour" of crisis response has been compressed into the "6-Minute Rule" due to the velocity of Twitter (X) and the prevalence of automated bot networks ("Electronic Flies").1 Furthermore, the definition of "crisis" in Saudi Arabia is inextricably linked to "Social Harmony" (Al-Silm Al-Ahli) and "National Image." Therefore, effective response strategies must integrate high-level strategic distance with deep digital immersion, a "Dual-Layer Approach" that secures narrative control while adhering to the stringent mandates of the Anti-Cybercrime Law.1
The transformation of the Saudi public sphere into a hyper-connected digital ecosystem has fundamentally altered the physics of reputation. In the pre-Vision 2030 era, information flow was relatively vertical and controlled. Today, it is horizontal, chaotic, and algorithmic. A single tweet from a disgruntled citizen regarding a delayed service in a remote province can be amplified by bot networks to trend globally within hours, forcing a ministerial-level response. This "butterfly effect" is the defining characteristic of the modern Saudi crisis landscape.
The following analysis is divided into nine strategic sectors, covering the theoretical failures of Western models in the GCC, the mechanics of digital escalation, detailed case study autopsies, and a prescriptive operational framework for establishing Command and Control in the age of algorithmic warfare. It provides a roadmap for transitioning from reactive firefighting to proactive reputational immunization.

2. The Saudi Operational Theater: Cultural and Digital Terrain Analysis
To operate effectively within the Kingdom, one must first map the terrain. The Saudi market is not a monolith; it is a high-context cultural environment overlaid with a hyper-modern digital infrastructure. This duality creates a unique "phygital" (physical + digital) reality where ancient social norms govern the newest digital platforms.
2.1 The Digital Majlis: Where Public Discourse Lives
Historically, the Majlis served as the physical space for community consensus, grievance airing, and tribal alignment. It was a space of controlled transparency, where leaders sat face-to-face with citizens to resolve disputes. Today, Twitter (X), Snapchat, and WhatsApp have digitized this function, but with a critical difference: the loss of physical intimacy and the gain of infinite scale.
The Saudi digital sphere operates as a modern, 24/7 Majlis. In this space, an attack on a brand or a ministry is not viewed merely as a consumer complaint; it is often framed as a breach of the social contract or an affront to collective dignity. Research indicates that the Saudi audience is "hyper-connected." A service failure in a government app (e.g., Absher or Tawakkalna) is not technically isolated; it is interpreted through the lens of national progress. If a digital service fails, it is often critiqued as a failure of the state’s modernization promise. Consequently, the threshold for what constitutes a "crisis" is significantly lower in KSA than in Western markets. A delayed flight or a culturally insensitive advertisement can trigger a Tier 2 Critical event within minutes due to this amplification effect.1
This "Digital Majlis" has its own unwritten rules. Just as one would not shout insults in a physical Majlis, there are expectations of decorum in the digital space. However, the anonymity of the internet often erodes these norms, leading to what sociologists might call "context collapse," where a private grievance becomes a public spectacle. The crisis manager must navigate this by treating the digital audience not as a faceless mob, but as a gathering of citizens expecting the same respect and responsiveness they would receive in a physical Majlis.
2.2 The Concept of "Wajh" (Face) and Collective Honor
A critical differentiator in the Saudi market is the cultural imperative of Wajh (Face). In Western crisis communication theory, particularly in the United States, the standard recommendation is often a blunt, public apology and a full admission of fault ("The Mea Culpa"). The logic is that transparency builds trust. However, in the Saudi context, this approach can backfire catastrophically.
* Dignity over Debate: Direct, combative denials are viewed as arrogant (Takabbur). Conversely, groveling apologies can be seen as weakness or a loss of institutional stature. A leader who apologizes too profusely may be perceived as incompetent rather than accountable. The JEDAR doctrine suggests a "Third Way": Clarification and Contextualization. The goal is to restore equilibrium without humiliating the entity or dismissing the validity of the public's anger.1
* The Nuance of Apology: When an apology is necessary, it must be phrased carefully. It should express regret for the "discomfort caused" or the "deviation from our standards" rather than a blanket admission of legal liability or moral failure. The apology must be dignified, reinforcing the institution's values while acknowledging the gap in delivery.
* Collective Responsibility: The COVID-19 pandemic solidified the concept of Kulluna Mas'ool ("We are all responsible"). Crisis messaging that appeals to this sense of shared national duty creates a psychological shield around the entity. The narrative must shift from "Company vs. Customer" to "Partners in National Progress." By framing the crisis as a shared challenge—"We are working together to improve this service for the nation"—the entity invites the public to be part of the solution rather than just critics.1
2.3 The Legal "Gravity": Weaponization of the Anti-Cybercrime Law
Unlike markets such as the US or UK, where "free speech" protections often shield aggressive consumer activism and even defamation, the Saudi crisis manager operates under the shadow of the strict Anti-Cybercrime Law. This legal framework fundamentally alters the response strategy, transforming it from a purely PR exercise into a Legal-PR hybrid operation.
* Criminalization of Rumors: Article 6 of the Anti-Cybercrime Law penalizes the production, preparation, transmission, or storage of material that impinges on public order, religious values, or public morals. Crucially, this includes the spread of rumors and false information. For a crisis manager, this is a powerful defensive tool. When an entity is targeted by fabricated news or malicious gossip, the response is not limited to a soft denial. It includes a formal reminder of these legal consequences. This effectively "chills" the spread of the rumor, as citizens are wary of legal repercussions for retweeting unverified information.1
* Privacy and Defamation: Article 3 criminalizes defamation and the invasion of privacy. This restricts the entity from releasing specific customer data to refute a claim (e.g., proving a customer was actually at fault) without careful legal review. In Western markets, a company might release a video log to prove a customer is lying. In Saudi Arabia, doing so could trigger a lawsuit for privacy violation. Corporate statements must be drafted to avoid inadvertent liability while still defending the brand’s reputation. This requires the "Bronze Team" in the crisis cell to have a dedicated Legal Liaison who reviews every tweet and press release.1
2.4 High-Context Communication Patterns
Saudi Arabia is a "High-Context" culture, meaning that communication relies heavily on implicit understanding, non-verbal cues (or in the digital realm, tone and emoji usage), and shared history. Low-context cultures (like the US or Germany) prefer direct, explicit messages.
* Indirectness as Strategy: In a crisis, a direct "No" or a flat denial can be perceived as aggressive. Effective Saudi crisis communication often employs indirect but firm language. Instead of saying "The accusation is false," a statement might read, "We would like to clarify the context regarding the circulated images..." This softens the confrontation while correcting the record.
* The Role of Emotion: Western corporate communication strives to be objective and dispassionate. Saudi communication values emotional resonance. A statement that sounds too robotic or corporate will be dismissed as "cold." The successful crisis response must convey care and concern ("We share your frustration," "We are pained by this incident") before moving to the facts. This emotional validation is the bridge that allows the facts to be heard.1

3. Crisis Frameworks: Western Theory vs. Saudi Reality
Standard academic frameworks for crisis management, such as Timothy Coombs’ Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT) or Fink’s Crisis Lifecycle, often fail when applied directly to the GCC region without localization. The cultural and digital velocity variables in Saudi Arabia render many of these models obsolete or counter-productive.
3.1 The Failure of SCCT in High-Context Cultures
SCCT suggests that organizations should match their response strategy to the level of crisis responsibility attributed to them by stakeholders. It categorizes strategies into "Denial" (we didn't do it), "Diminish" (it's not that bad), and "Rebuild" (apology and compensation).
* The Mismatch: SCCT assumes a linear attribution of blame based on facts. In Saudi Arabia, blame is often fluid and can be heavily influenced by external factors such as "Electronic Flies" (inorganic amplification), tribal loyalties, or geopolitical alignment. A "Rebuild" strategy (apology + compensation) might be interpreted not as a responsible corporate move, but as an admission of systemic incompetence. If an entity apologizes too quickly for a minor issue, it might signal that it is weak and prone to failure, inviting further attacks.
* The "Diminish" Danger: In a culture of honor, trying to "diminish" a crisis—saying "it's just a small error"—can be seen as insulting the Wajh of the affected party. If a customer feels slighted, telling them the issue is minor is a second insult.
* The Adaptation: The JEDAR doctrine replaces these linear responses with a "Dual-Layer Approach":
   1. Layer 1 (Deep Immersion): Embedding into the digital "street" to understand the vernacular, memes, and specific grievances. This is about emotional intelligence—reading the room before speaking.
   2. Layer 2 (Strategic Distance): Maintaining a high-level view to ensure tactical concessions do not compromise long-term strategic goals (e.g., Vision 2030 alignment) or National Image. This balance allows the entity to respond with empathy (Layer 1) while maintaining authority (Layer 2).1
3.2 The Velocity Gap: The "Golden Hour" vs. The "6-Minute Rule"
Traditional PR teaches the "Golden Hour"—the first 60 minutes after a crisis breaks where an organization has the chance to set the narrative. In the Saudi digital ecosystem, the Golden Hour is a relic of a slower age.
* The 6-Minute Reality: Analysis of X (Twitter) dynamics in KSA suggests that a definitive narrative frame is established within the first 6 minutes of a viral spike. If the entity remains silent during this window, an "Information Vacuum" is created.
* Vacuum Dynamics: In the absence of an official holding statement, this vacuum is not empty; it is immediately filled by:
   * Rumors and speculation: "I heard the CEO fled," "The system was hacked."
   * Hostile interpretations by "Electronic Flies": State-sponsored or competitor-sponsored bot networks that seize on the vacuum to push a narrative of failure or corruption.
   * Competitor-driven narratives: Rivals subtly highlighting their own stability.
* Operational Implication: This extreme velocity means that the traditional bureaucratic approval chain—where a statement goes from a PR manager to a VP, then to Legal, then to the CEO—is fatal. By the time the statement is approved (2 hours later), the reputation damage is already calcified.
* The Solution: Crisis teams must be pre-authorized to release "Holding Statements" within this 6-minute window. These templates should be pre-approved by Legal and the CEO before a crisis ever happens, requiring only the insertion of specific details. This allows the team to seize the narrative initiative immediately.1
3.3 The "Stabilizing Anchor" Philosophy
JEDAR introduces the concept of the "Stabilizing Anchor." In the chaos of a Saudi crisis—whether it be a crowd control incident during Hajj or a cybersecurity breach—the immediate reaction of leadership is often emotional, driven by the fear of reputational damage. This panic can lead to knee-jerk reactions, such as firing an employee publicly or issuing a defensive denial.
The "Stabilizing Anchor" philosophy mandates that the crisis management team (specifically the Silver Commander) acts as a counterbalance to this emotion. By relying on data (sentiment analysis, velocity metrics) rather than "gut feeling," the team provides a calm, strategic assessment. They remind the Gold Commander (CEO/Minister) that "this too shall pass" and that the response must be calculated to restore the institution to its natural state of strength, not just to make the noise stop.1

4. Digital Escalation Insights: The Anatomy of a Saudi Twitter Storm
Understanding the mechanics of digital escalation is critical for distinguishing between genuine public outcry and coordinated attacks. The Saudi digital sphere is a battlefield where organic sentiment is often weaponized by inorganic actors.
4.1 The "Electronic Flies" (Dhubab Iliktrūniya)
A unique threat vector in the GCC is the presence of coordinated bot networks, often originating from hostile regional actors. These networks are designed to sow discord, attack national projects (like NEOM, Riyadh Season, or major infrastructure), and amplify negative sentiment against specific entities.1
Identification Protocols
Distinguishing between a real angry customer and a bot is the first step in triage.

The JEDAR doctrine advises strictly against engaging with these accounts.
* The Trap: Replying to a bot, even to debunk it, validates the bot’s existence. It signals to the platform's algorithm that this account is generating "meaningful interaction," which boosts its visibility.
* The Maneuver: The correct strategy is "Block Without Comment" combined with "Flooding the Zone." The entity should block the bot accounts to remove them from its notification stream and comments section. Simultaneously, it must release a high volume of positive, SEO-optimized content (news, success stories, community initiatives) to push the bot-generated negative content down the timeline and search results.1
4.2 The Role of Influencers and "Wasta" (Connections)
In the Saudi market, official channels are often viewed with a degree of skepticism ("It's just PR"), while individual influencers (Key Opinion Leaders - KOLs) command significant trust.
* Soft Power Activation: During a crisis, the "Silver Commander" (Crisis Director) must leverage personal networks (Wasta) to backchannel the "real story" to influential journalists and KOLs. This allows the entity to seed its narrative informally. For example, while the official account posts a formal holding statement, a trusted KOL can tweet, "I've spoken to sources inside [Entity], and the reality is actually [Context]." This "soft power" is often faster and more credible than official press releases.1
* The Influencer Risk: Conversely, influencers are a major source of risk. A contracted influencer behaving inappropriately at a heritage site or mocking local customs can trigger a Tier 3 Cultural Crisis. The "Riyadh Comedy Festival" backlash is a prime example where content deemed culturally insensitive sparked a firestorm.
* The Solution: Contracts with influencers must include strict "Morality Clauses" and "Crisis Protocols." If an influencer missteps, the entity must immediately dissociate itself, issuing a statement that reaffirms national values and confirms that the individual’s views do not reflect the organization’s principles.1
4.3 The "Hashtag Hijack" Phenomenon
Hostile actors often "hijack" trending hashtags to inject negative messaging. For example, during a positive event like #RiyadhSeason, bots might flood the hashtag with unrelated complaints or fabricated scandals to dilute the positive sentiment.
* Defense: "Hashtag Occupation." For predictable events, the crisis team must "occupy" the digital space early. This means populating the hashtag with thousands of pieces of positive content (images, videos, user testimonials) before the event starts. This creates a "content fortress" that makes it harder for negative content to gain traction.1

5. Crisis Typology and Case Studies
To move from theory to practice, we must analyze specific crisis tiers using the JEDAR classification matrix.1 Each tier requires a distinct operational cadence.
5.1 Tier 4: National Security & Mass Public Safety (The Hajj Model)
Definition: Events threatening national security, Hajj/Umrah operations, international relations, or public health.
* Case Study Context: Hajj Crowd Control & Logistics
   * The Challenge: The massive scale of Hajj (millions of pilgrims moving in confined spaces) presents constant risks of stampedes, health hazards (like MERS or COVID), or transport failures. A breakdown here is not just a logistical failure; it is a reputational blow to the Kingdom’s custodianship of the Two Holy Mosques.1
   * Success Factors: The integration of the Unified Security Operations Center (911) and the National Center for Security Operations (NCSO) allows for real-time data fusion. When a crisis occurs (e.g., a transport delay at a jamming point), the response is multi-agency (Ministry of Interior, Ministry of Hajj, Health, Media).
   * Narrative Strategy: The narrative focuses on the state’s "tireless efforts" to serve the Guests of Rahman. Transparency is balanced with reassurance. The messaging emphasizes that the state is deploying all resources to resolve the issue, framing the incident as a challenge being overcome by collective national will. The "hero" of the story is the security officer serving water to pilgrims, shifting the focus from the delay to the service.1
   * Marcom Integration: For events like the Hajj and Umrah Conference, PR teams develop detailed "Crisis and Disaster Management Plans" that include specific risk assessments for cyberattacks, negative media campaigns, and technical failures. This pre-planning ensures that if an incident occurs, the media center can instantly pivot from "Promotion Mode" to "Containment Mode" without losing a beat.4
5.2 Tier 3: Dangerous/Brand Threat (The "Hamburgini" Incident)
Definition: Threats to life, significant financial loss, or major legal/corruption violations.
* Case Study: Hamburgini Food Poisoning
   * The Incident: A mass food poisoning outbreak linked to the popular local chain "Hamburgini" in Riyadh.
   * The Escalation: The crisis moved rapidly from individual complaints on Twitter to a trending national topic, involving hospitalization reports and official Ministry of Health interventions.
   * The Breakdown: The crisis dragged on, creating "Fatigue Management" issues for the crisis team. Initial silence or delayed responses allowed rumors to fester about the cause (e.g., spoiled meat vs. mayonnaise).
   * The Recovery (Reform Phase): The key to recovery in such cases is "Visible Change." It is not enough to apologize; the entity must demonstrate structural reform. This involves publishing videos of new hygiene protocols, announcing third-party audits, and potentially changing leadership or suppliers publicly. The public must see the correction, not just hear about it.
   * Comparative Insight: Unlike Western brands that might litigate liability for years to avoid payouts (e.g., the McDonald's coffee case), the Saudi market demands immediate moral accountability. "Goodwill Initiatives"—such as offering free meals or supporting health charities—are essential to restore public affection once safety is assured.1
5.3 Tier 2: Critical/Cultural Missteps (The "Al Baik" Pivot)
Definition: Incidents threatening brand reputation or rumors gaining viral traction.
* Case Study: Al Baik's Philanthropic Armor
   * Context: Al Baik is a beloved "Love Brand" in KSA. However, it faces operational challenges (long queues, supply chain issues) and occasional rumors like any major chain.
   * The Strategy: Al Baik has built a massive "Reputation Buffer" through decades of community service, charity, and alignment with national values. They rarely engage in aggressive marketing; their brand is built on humility and service.
   * The Outcome: When they face a minor crisis or operational delay, the public often defends the brand spontaneously ("Don't attack Al Baik, they do so much for us"). This is the ultimate goal of the "Immunization" phase in the JEDAR cycle—creating a reservoir of goodwill so deep that the brand becomes antifragile. A crisis that would destroy a newer brand merely scratches the surface of Al Baik.1
   * Lesson: Crisis management begins years before the crisis. It is built on the "Brand Bank Account" principle—you must make deposits (goodwill) before you can make a withdrawal (crisis forgiveness).
5.4 Tier 1: Routine/Digital Hygiene (The SEO "Silent Crisis")
Definition: Minor operational errors, low-traction negative sentiment, or "Digital Invisibility."
* Insight from Parallel Markets (Egyptian Food Bank Proxy):
   * The Egyptian Food Bank SEO Report 5 highlights a critical vulnerability applicable to Saudi entities: Keyword Hijacking.
   * The Scenario: A major Saudi charity or NGO relies on its brand name but neglects generic SEO.
   * The Crisis: During Ramadan, users search for "Donate Zakat KSA" or "Iftar Sa'em." Because the official entity has poor SEO (single-page structure, heavy JS, no intent-based hubs), it does not rank. Instead, the search results are dominated by news aggregators, commercial retailers (selling food boxes), or potentially fraudulent sites.
   * The Impact: This is a "Silent Crisis" of lost revenue and lost trust. The entity is "invisible" at the moment of highest intent.
   * The Solution: "Digital Immunization" involves creating a robust "Content Architecture" (Landing Pages, Hubs for Zakat/Hajj/Donation) that occupies the search engine results page (SERP). This pushes negative news or competitor sites off the first page, effectively burying potential reputational threats before they are seen. It transforms the website from a "brochure" into a "Donation Engine".5

6. Detailed Operational Protocols: The Command Center
In the event of a Tier 3 or 4 crisis, decentralized decision-making is a liability. The JEDAR Playbook mandates the activation of a Crisis Command Center (War Room).1 This is not a metaphor; it requires a physical location and a dedicated digital infrastructure.
6.1 Team Structure: The Crisis Cell
The structure follows a Gold-Silver-Bronze command hierarchy to ensure speed and strategic alignment.
* Gold Commander (Strategic):
   * Role: CEO, Minister, or Chairman.
   * Authority: Sets the ultimate objective (e.g., "Protect the Brand," "Ensure Public Safety," "Minimize Legal Exposure"). Authorizes high-level financial resources and approves the final wording of holding statements involving national policy. They do not get involved in drafting tweets or choosing fonts.
* Silver Commander (Tactical):
   * Role: VP of Communications or Crisis Director.
   * Authority: Manages War Room operations. Coordinates the JEDAR task force. Approves specific messaging tactics (e.g., which influencer to activate, which media outlet to leak to). Ensures the "6-Minute Rule" is met. Acts as the "Stabilizing Anchor."
* Bronze Teams (Operational):
   * Monitoring Unit: Uses AI tools (Brandwatch, Sprinklr) to track sentiment velocity. Detects "Patient Zero" of rumors. Reports directly to the Silver Commander every 15 minutes.1
   * Content Unit: Rapid-response creatives. They draft statements, design infographics to clarify facts, and edit video clarifications in real-time. They must have pre-approved templates for every asset type.
   * Legal Liaison: Critical role. Ensures all communications comply with the Anti-Cybercrime Law and Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL). Prevents the team from admitting liability that could lead to prosecution. Checks every word for "defamation" risks.1
   * Liaison Officer: The link to external bodies like the Center for Government Communication (CGC) to ensure narrative alignment with the state’s unified messaging ("Singing from the same song sheet"). This is vital for government or semi-gov entities.1
6.2 The Digital Tech Stack
To function at the "speed of Twitter," the Command Center requires integrated technology.1 Manual monitoring is impossible in a viral storm.
* Listening: Brandwatch or Sprinklr for volume tracking and sentiment analysis. These tools can identify the geolocation of tweets (to spot foreign bot farms).
* Coordination: CrisisGo or Everbridge for immediate team communication, protocol activation, and mass notification. These apps ensure that every team member has the crisis plan on their phone, accessible offline.
* Visual Forensics: Amazon Rekognition is vital for the modern crisis. It can identify unauthorized use of entity logos in fake news, analyze visual sentiment in viral videos, and detect deepfakes. If a fake video of a CEO saying something offensive circulates, Rekognition can help prove it is AI-generated within minutes.1
* Moderation: Google Perspective API or Microsoft Content Moderator to filter toxic comments automatically. This prevents the entity’s own social media pages from becoming a toxic battleground where users abuse each other.

7. Strategic Messaging & Communication Protocols
7.1 The Syntax of the Holding Statement
A holding statement in KSA must balance authority with empathy. It is not just a placeholder; it is the first line of defense. It must be released within 15 minutes of verification (The 6-Minute Rule applies to acknowledgment, 15 minutes to statement).
* Template for Rumor Rebuttal (Cyber Law Focus):"The [Entity Name] categorically denies the rumors circulating regarding. We confirm that operations are proceeding normally. We remind the public that creating or sharing fabricated information is a violation of Article 6 of the Anti-Cybercrime Law. Legal measures will be taken against the sources of these fabrications." 1
   * Analysis: This statement does two things: it denies the rumor and immediately pivots to a legal threat, leveraging the state’s authority to silence the "Electronic Flies." It shifts the frame from "Is the rumor true?" to "Am I breaking the law by retweeting this?"
* Template for Operational Failure (Empathy Focus):"We are aware of the delay at [Location] and our specialized teams are currently on-site. The safety of [Guests/Citizens] is our absolute priority. We apologize for this inconvenience and are working to restore service within." 1
   * Analysis: This validates the user's pain (preventing anger escalation) and provides a concrete action plan. It avoids making excuses ("The weather was bad") and focuses on the solution.
7.2 Tone of Voice: "Royal" vs. "Human"
The tone must be calibrated to the platform and the audience.
* Official Statements (Press Release/Website): Must use "Royal" or "Formal" Arabic (Fusha). This conveys seriousness, dignity, and state authority. It signals that the situation is under control.
* Social Media Engagement (Twitter Replies): Can be slightly softer, but never colloquial or slangy during a crisis.
* Religious Alignment: Critical statements, especially those involving safety, fatalities, or national events, must begin with Bismillah ("In the name of God") and use appropriate religious terminology (e.g., Rahimahullah for the deceased). This aligns the entity with the spiritual values of the public and the leadership. A secular-sounding statement in a moment of tragedy can be seen as alien and disconnected.1
* Unity-Based Language: Use collective terminology ("Our Nation," "Our Sons and Daughters," "The Guests of God"). This invokes the Kulluna Mas'ool spirit, framing the crisis resolution as a patriotic duty rather than a corporate transaction. It reminds the audience that "we are on the same side".1
7.3 Persuasion Psychology
Drawing from general copywriting principles 6, the messaging should use "Consistency" and "Authority" to persuade the public to accept the entity's narrative.
* Consistency: "As we have always been committed to Vision 2030..." (Leveraging past commitments to frame current actions). "We promised transparency, and here are the facts."
* Authority: Citing partnerships with the Ministry of Interior, the CGC, or recognized religious bodies (like Dar Al-Ifta for Zakat issues) lends the entity the weight of the state/religion. It moves the argument from "The Company says" to "The Authorities say".6

8. Case Simulations (War Gaming)
To ensure readiness, the JEDAR doctrine utilizes detailed simulations ("War Games") based on probable threats. These allow the team to practice the protocols before the real fire starts.1
Scenario A: The "Fabricated Leak" (Cyber/Rumor)
* Situation: A doctored PDF document bearing the entity’s official letterhead circulates on WhatsApp and X. It claims that a popular government service app (e.g., Absher or Tawakkalna) has been hacked and millions of user records (including photos) have been leaked.
* Escalation: The hashtag #Absher_Hack trends within 10 minutes. Panic spreads; users try to delete the app.
* JEDAR Response Protocol:
   1. T+0 mins: AI monitoring (Brandwatch) detects a spike in keywords "Hack," "Leak," "Data," "Breach." Alert sent to Bronze Monitoring Team.
   2. T+15 mins: The Technical Team verifies system integrity (confirms Negative Breach).
   3. T+30 mins: The Silver Commander authorizes the issuance of a "Denial & Caution" statement. This statement explicitly cites the Anti-Cybercrime Law penalties for spreading rumors to deter further sharing.
   4. T+60 mins: Release of a technical infographic showing the "Green" security status and explaining the visual signs that the leaked document is fake (e.g., wrong font, old logo).
   5. T+2 hours: The Liaison Officer coordinates with the CGC to have key influencers and other government accounts retweet the official denial. The Legal Team files a formal report against the originators of the document.
   6. Recovery: A campaign on "Data Safety" is launched the following week to reassure users.1
Scenario B: The "Influencer Misstep" (Cultural)
* Situation: A contracted influencer for a new tourism project (e.g., AlUla or Red Sea) posts a video on Snapchat. In the background, they are seen dancing inappropriately near a heritage site or wearing clothing deemed offensive to conservative norms.
* Escalation: Conservative Twitter segments amplify the video. The narrative shifts to "This project is destroying our values."
* JEDAR Response Protocol:
   1. Immediate Action: The Silver Commander orders the suspension of the influencer's contract privately.
   2. Public Action (T+45 mins): Issue a statement emphasizing that individual views/actions do not reflect the entity's values. Importantly, do not repost or link to the offensive content to avoid the "Streisand Effect."
   3. Corrective Action: Launch a counter-campaign highlighting local heritage, respect, and community engagement. Feature local elders or community leaders praising the project.
   4. Legal: Review the contract's "Morality Clause" for damages.1

10. Strategic Recovery & The "Right to Be Forgotten"
The crisis does not end when the hashtag stops trending. The Recovery Phase is where true resilience is built.
10.1 The "Reform" Phase
If the crisis was caused by an internal fault (e.g., the Hamburgini poisoning), the entity must visibly demonstrate the correction.
* Visible Change: Publish videos of new hygiene protocols.
* Third-Party Validation: Announce audits by recognized international bodies.
* Leadership Renewal: Introduce new leadership if necessary to signal a "clean slate." 1
10.2 SEO & Digital Hygiene (The "Right to be Forgotten")
The battle for information superiority moves to search engines.
* Positive Displacement: Launching a sustained content campaign (articles, videos, press releases) optimized for the keywords associated with the crisis. The goal is to push negative news results off the first page of Google, effectively "burying" the crisis in the archives.
* The "Digital Ghost" Risk: As seen in the Egyptian Food Bank analysis, if an entity does not populate its own keywords, others will. Post-crisis, the entity must aggressively optimize for terms like " Scandal" or " News" with positive, factual content to control the narrative for future searchers.5
* Wikipedia & Database Audits: Ensuring factual accuracy is restored on all encyclopedic platforms so the crisis does not become the defining feature of the entity's history.1
10.3 Post-Crisis Evaluation (KPIs)
Measuring success is not just about survival. JEDAR uses specific KPIs to evaluate the response:
* Response Time: Was the first acknowledgment issued within 15 minutes?
* Sentiment Shift: How long did it take for sentiment to return to the pre-crisis baseline?
* Misinformation Correction Rate: What percentage of viral rumors were successfully debunked?
* Audience Retention: Did we lose followers/customers, or did we gain respect through transparency? 1

Crisis management in the Saudi market is a high-stakes discipline that sits at the intersection of statecraft, law, and tribal culture. It demands a response architecture that is rigid in its command structure (The War Room) yet fluid in its cultural engagement (The Majlis).
